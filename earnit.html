EarnIt

Client:DESIGNATION

Date:April 15, 2016

Role:UX & UI

For this mock project, my team was tasked with creating a digital product that reimagines how people can adopt and maintain a healthy lifestyle. During three one-week UX sprints and three one-week UI sprints, we designed a system that rewards users for turning daily walking into exercise.

Exploration

Finding a forest amongst the trees

We explored trends in the fitness, food, and health industries through secondary domain research, surveys, and user interviews. We asked people to define health and fitness, discuss their exercise and eating routines, and share recent experiences related to health and fitness. Many discussed how lack of time prevented them from meeting their health goals.

“Time has always been my biggest obstacle. It’s hard to fit in gym-time when I’m working—you know—14-hour days.” – Interviewee

Getting to know Sarah

We employed personas to model behavioral and attitudinal trends. Sarah, our primary persona, wants to exercise more throughout the week. She hikes on weekends, but her weekdays are too busy to fit in exercise. Sarah was our litmus test for design concoctions to ensure that our process was user-centric.

Our Vision

Time is of the Essence

Sarah also helped us personalize the core problem that we elected to focus on.

Sarah believes she is unable to take care of her physical well-being because she does not have enough time to exercise.

People wanted to exercise more, but were not finding time in their busy schedules. Our survey results showed that people use exercise as their primary form of stress relief, but were still dissatisfied with their current level of fitness.

There are two key assumptions that underlie Sarah’s problem:

  1. She doesn’t have time in her schedule to exercise.
  2. Exercise is time-intensive.
How it’s done

While the core problem defines what our interface must solve, we also envisioned how it would accomplish this. We thus developed some musts.

Our solution must be:
  • Straightforward: Simplify choices and strive for clear language.
  • Warm: Provide encouragement and convey trustworthiness.
  • Delightful: Make unexpected joy the norm.
  • Time Efficient: Offer quick and easy solutions.
  • Harmonious: integrate seamlessly into daily life.

People we interviewed were looking for ways to be more active without adding another task.

Where We Landed

Our Latest Iteration

EarnIt is an mobile application that rewards you for moving more. Select a sponsor at the beginning of each month and earn $1 credit for each day that you walk 10,000-steps. Use EarnIt to redeem your credit in-store or online.

Amateur no more

Users can choose from a list of premium brand sponsors for your store credit. Brands like Nike, Adidas, Patagonia, The North Face, and Columbia.

Versatile tracking

Users can pair EarnIt with most wearables or can simply track with their smartphone’s accelerometer.

Celebrate

Reach 10,000 steps each day and see your earnings increase.

Redeem with your phone

Digital gift cards are stored in-app and can be redeemed in-store or online.

Our Journey

The path to our latest iteration taught me a lot about iterative process. Inside a 6-week timeline, we spent five weeks developing an solution that failed user testing and one week pivoting to a new concept solution. In order to meet our deadline with a solution that held our confidence, we had to be decisive.

Navigating a Concept Sea

With a problem defined, we brainstormed a few concepts.

Early...

Using sketching and POP, we employed early user testing to evaluate the clarity and desirability of our initial concept solutions.

  • Clarity: Does the concept have a clear and cohesive function?
  • Desirability: Does the concept align with users goals?

Testing revealed the strongest desirability for and clearest understanding of FitAssist. FitAssist uses smart calendar integration to help the user schedule exercise into her week. By accessing the user's digital calendar, it finds opportunities to exercise and prompts the user to schedule. Using self-administered rewards, the app reminds the user of their weekly exercise goal and the upcoming reward.

and Often

With initial data in-hand, we created a mid-fidelity prototype in Axure. Because of our short timeframe, we then rushed into designing a high-fidelity UI without testing the mid-fidelity prototype.

Note-to-self: If you build an interactive prototype, test it.

PlanIt

Gamify

We explored various UI styles, but decided to move forward with the UI concept that we initially felt best reflected our design principles. To visually link goal-achievement to rewards, we created a space travel theme that modeled the user's progress toward his/her goal. Testers found the experience “fun and delightful,” but were skeptical that it would motivate them to exercise. PlanIt required high-user input, which is both time-consuming and cognitively burdensome. This confirmed that we needed to rethink the underlying concept of self-set goals and rewards.

The Problem with User Testing

Late-stage user testing revealed desirability issues that had not come up during low-fidelity prototype testing. Testers struggled to create rewards and felt that the app was little more useful than their personal calendar. Additionally, many were not confident that they'd be able to withold rewards. Why wasn't this revealed in earlier concept testing?

In early testing, we asked testers to look at paper prototypes and imagine themselves using the app. But we didn't tell them when to stop imagining. When imagining full functionality of the app, they also idealized their behaviors related to the app.

For example, in low-fidelity testing, we asked testers to imagine scheduling activities for the week, but didn't ask them to explicitly share those activities aloud. Had we asked them to share them aloud, they might have hesitated or shown uncertainty about that requirement.

Is Self-Rewarding Actually Rewarding?

Leveraging self-reward is tricky because it offers the opportunity of choice burden of choice on the user.

Many studies suggest that setting effective rewards is particularly difficult when the goals and associated tasks require a lot of self-control. Choosing to exercise over watching an episode of Orange is the New Black requires self-control. Add the burden of resisting your end-of-week reward and suddenly the whole experience is burdensome.

Given these major challenges, we decided that we needed to pivot.

The Pivot

Brand-sponsorship

Self-rewarding was too burdensome for our users, so we came up with a new goal and reward system. We knew that earning or saving money can be an effective motivator, but offering cash to users was unfeasible. We settled on brand-sponsorship to motivate users by offering gift-cards for exercising. Brand-sponsorship could increase brand-awareness, strengthen brand-loyalty, and improve brand-image.

Using an external reward system forced us to shift to a standardized goal system. We decided to adopt the 10,000-step model, which is both familiar and a simple strategy for increasing the frequency of physical activity inside a busy schedule. While major brands primarily use social-competition, monetary-reward systems are absent in the general fitness market; such systems are only available through employee health programs.

Paradigm Shift

By chance, pivoting to the 10,000-step model actually solves Sarah's time-problem in a new way. Recall Sarah's two assumptions:

  1. She doesn’t have time in her schedule to exercise.
  2. Exercise is time-consuming.

Both FitAssist and PlanIt were attempts to tackle the first assumption by revealing opportunities in her schedule to exercise, but they required too much user input. EarnIt tackles the second assumption by framing walking as a exercise and by motivating her with external rewards.

It was all a dream

Did we innovate?

The benefits of engaging in a project with no stakeholder restrictions is that it allowed us to explore a wide breadth of possibilities. Although elements of our solution are not individually new—the 10,000-step model, afterall, has existed for decades—we believe the combination of elements are innovative.

Some lessons I learned

  1. Problem first. It was tempting to search for the solution during exploratory user interviews or to ask users for the solution. But if you start searching for solutions before clearly defining the problem, you're not likely to help anyone.

  2. Test early and often. Ideal testing involves users that match your user profile, but testing internally can help reveal issues quickly.

  3. Quadruple diamond. One round of exploring the problem space and then one round of brainstorming is rarely enough. We completed two full rounds of exploratory research and three distinct brainstorming sprints.
